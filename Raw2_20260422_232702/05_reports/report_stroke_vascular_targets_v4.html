This report identifies stroke-specific surface proteins on brain endothelial cells (ECs) that are upregulated in the ischemic hemisphere and suitable as targeting antigens for IV-administered nanoparticles or cell therapy vectors. Five transcriptomic datasets were analyzed (3 primary vascular-enriched + 2 confirmatory), and all 78 candidate genes were systematically evaluated for three critical targeting criteria: surface accessibility from the bloodstream, brain EC enrichment vs. other cell types, and confirmed upregulation after stroke.

Key findings — v3 re-curation
  • Only 9 genes (9) meet all three criteria for viable nanoparticle targeting antigens (Classes A–C).
  • 41 genes are intracellular or secreted — they appear in stroke transcriptomics but are not accessible from the bloodstream and should not be pursued as targeting antigens.
  • Top 4 targets (Class A): ECSCR, CD93, SELE (E-selectin), TNFRSF12A (Fn14) — all confirmed transmembrane proteins with extracellular domains accessible from the luminal surface, upregulated in brain ECs after stroke.
  • SELE is the only target already validated as a nanoparticle targeting antigen in stroke models (ES-bp peptide).
  • ECSCR is the most EC-specific target (EC-exclusive expression) and is upregulated in all 3 primary vascular datasets.
  • ACKR1 (Duffy/DARC) is human-validated (GSE9391, p=0.023) but also expressed on erythrocytes — off-target binding risk for IV nanoparticles.

7. Immune Cell Extravasation: Dual-Use Potential of EC Surface Targets

After ischemic stroke, brain endothelial cells upregulate a cascade of surface molecules that mediate the recruitment of peripheral immune cells (neutrophils, monocytes, T cells) across the BBB into the ischemic parenchyma [82,106,112]. This neuroinflammatory response is initially protective (debris clearance) but becomes damaging within hours to days [114,115]. Several of the 9 Class A–C surface targets identified here are not merely passive targeting handles — they are active participants in the leukocyte extravasation cascade. This creates a dual-use opportunity: nanoparticles targeted to these molecules can simultaneously deliver therapeutic cargo AND modulate immune infiltration.

The leukocyte extravasation cascade at the BBB Leukocyte recruitment proceeds through sequential steps [95,152,155]: (1) Tethering/rolling — selectins (SELE/SELP) capture leukocytes from flow; (2) Activation — chemokines (CXCL1, CCL2) activate leukocyte integrins; (3) Firm adhesion — ICAM-1/VCAM-1 bind activated β2-integrins (Mac-1, LFA-1); (4) Crawling — leukocytes migrate to junctions; (5) Transmigration (diapedesis) — paracellular (via PECAM-1/CD99) or transcellular crossing. Targeting molecules at Steps 1 or 3 has the greatest impact on leukocyte infiltration.

7.2 Dual-Use Targeting Strategies

Strategy 1 — SELE-targeted nanoparticles (highest dual-use value) E-selectin is the canonical leukocyte rolling receptor and the most validated stroke EC target. Anti-SELE nanoparticles (using ES-bp peptide or anti-SELE mAb) have been shown to accumulate >10-fold more in inflamed brain vasculature than anti-ICAM-1 or anti-TfR1 nanoparticles [88]. Loading these nanoparticles with anti-inflammatory cargo (e.g., dexamethasone, IL-10, siRNA against ICAM-1/VCAM-1) creates a self-reinforcing therapeutic: the nanoparticle targets the inflamed EC, delivers cargo that reduces further leukocyte rolling, and thereby limits secondary neuroinflammatory injury [88,92,95].
Strategy 2 — TNFRSF12A (Fn14)-targeted nanoparticles (upstream immunomodulation) Fn14 is an upstream master regulator of the EC adhesion molecule programme. TWEAK/Fn14 signalling drives NF-κB-dependent transcription of ICAM-1, VCAM-1, and E-selectin [87,97]. Targeting Fn14 with nanoparticles loaded with NF-κB inhibitors or Fn14-blocking antibodies could simultaneously deliver cargo to stroke ECs AND reduce the entire downstream leukocyte recruitment programme — a single-target approach with broad anti-inflammatory impact.
Strategy 3 — PLAUR (uPAR)-targeted nanoparticles (caution required) EC-expressed uPAR is upregulated after stroke and can be targeted with AE105 peptide or anti-uPAR mAb (ATN-658). However, uPAR on circulating leukocytes is required for their own β2-integrin-mediated transmigration [149]. Systemic uPAR blockade could impair immune surveillance. Nanoparticle-based targeting (which does not block uPAR function) avoids this concern — the nanoparticle binds EC-uPAR for delivery without occupying leukocyte-uPAR. This distinction is critical for therapeutic design.
Strategy 4 — NT5E (CD73)-targeted nanoparticles (enzymatic activity must be preserved) CD73 generates extracellular adenosine, which suppresses neutrophil and T-cell recruitment via A2A receptors [112]. Post-stroke CD73 upregulation is likely a counter-regulatory mechanism. Targeting CD73 with blocking antibodies (e.g., oleclumab, used in oncology) would remove this adenosine brake and could worsen neuroinflammation. Non-blocking targeting ligands (e.g., nanobodies that bind CD73 without inhibiting its enzymatic activity) are required for safe nanoparticle delivery via this receptor.

12. Caveats and Limitations

#LimitationImpactMitigation
1Mouse-to-human translation All primary datasets are murine MCAO models. GSE9391 provides partial human validation (SELE, ACKR1). Wälchli 2024 (GSE256490) human brain vascular atlas not yet processed.
2Model heterogeneity Three different MCAO models (permanent, photothrombotic, transient). Target expression may differ with reperfusion. Cross-dataset consensus reduces model-specific artifacts. Targets in all 3 models are most robust.
3ECSCR stroke confirmation ECSCR is upregulated in all 3 primary datasets but direct stroke-specific confirmation in brain ECs is limited in the literature. Strong transcriptional evidence across 3 independent datasets. Needs in vitro/in vivo protein-level confirmation.
4APOLD1 stroke confirmation APOLD1 is vascular-restricted but direct stroke-specific upregulation data is limited. Upregulated in pathological angiogenesis conditions; ischemia-responsive. Needs direct stroke confirmation.
5GSE234052 pseudoreplication Wilcoxon tests on individual cells (not pseudobulk) due to absent replicate metadata. Treated as exploratory; GSE223744 and GSE225948 (pseudobulk DESeq2) are statistically more rigorous.
6ACKR1 erythrocyte expression ACKR1 is expressed on erythrocytes — IV nanoparticles would bind circulating red blood cells. ACKR1 classified as Class D despite human validation. Not recommended as primary targeting antigen for IV delivery.
